An Analysis of the Malaysia's 15th General Election

Malaysia's 15th General Election (GE15) was held on Nov 19, 2022. It was the first General Election which allows Malaysians who are 18 years old or above to vote. The election comes amidst a season of political turmoil (three prime ministers in four years), which followed the watershed May 2018 elections that saw the ruling coalition lose power for the first time since independence. The political landscape has made this election particularly unpredictable. The aim of my project is to give an overview of Malaysiaâ€™s 15th General Election.

Age, Gender and Ethnicity of Candidates Running for Malaysia's 15th General Election

In this section, we break down every GE15 candidates by their age, gender and ethnicity. Data is downloaded directly from the Malaysia General Election Website. After uploading the data onto Github, the data is vizualized through Vega.js.

Malaysia has a higher ratio of female parliamentarians than many other developing countries. However, there is still a wide gender gap in the representation of women in Malaysian politics as majority tend to think men make better leaders. Thus, from the chart above, we can see that male candidates dominate the election landscape.

Bumiputera candidates are the clear majority because they are often seen as having closer ties to traditional Malay culture, which can further increase their chances of winning. Constituency-level ethnic structure is a very robust and consistent predictor of which coalition prevailed in elections on the peninsula.

Although the voting age was lowered to 18, the median age of candidates is still within the 50 to 60 years old range.

Ethnicity and GE15 Results

As mentioned just now, it is important to look through the results on the peninsula to get a sense of how ethnicity structures Malaysian politics. Here, I compare the vote share for each coalition with the bumiputera population share within that constituency. Blue points are where the coalition in question lost; red points are where they won. The deep structure of peninsular Malaysian politics jumps right out. There is one coalition that fares well in non-Malay majority constituencies. And the other two fare well in Malay-majority constituencies. We can see that the battleground is the mixed constituencies on the peninsula with 20-40% non-bumiputera populations. Rejected or Spoilt Votes

This is a choropleth map that shows the percentage of spoilt or rejected votes for each parliamentary seat. According to the Election Commission of Malaysia, rejected votes do not count towards the total number of votes cast in the election, and therefore can have a significant impact on the outcome of the election. This chart is chosen because it allows for more intuitive and quicker interpretation of the data. We can see that rural areas, such as the north of the peninsula, tend to have a higher rate of rejected or spoilt votes. Data is downloaded from the Malaysia General Election Website. Python is used to construct this chart which can be found here.

Total Seats Won by each political parties in GE15

The chart above shows the total seats won by political parties that contested in GE15. The ruling coalition, Barisan Nasional lost its majority, coming in third with 30 seats. Perikatan Nasional, a coalition of conservative and Islamist parties led by former Prime Minister Muhyiddin Yassin, came in second with 74 seats while Pakatan Harapan coalition won the most with 82 seats.

Data and Challenges faced

This webpage is built using HTML, CSS and Javascript. It uses the vega-lite Javascript API for constructing simple and interactive visualizations. Before building the front-end, the data is downloaded for preprocessing. All data is sourced from the Malaysia General Election Website.

Most of the challenges faced during this stage came from turning the data into something that can be used with ease. This involves removing unnecessary columns, transforming the data into the desired format, and translating certain data from Malay to English. Futhermore, some data must also be derived manually by performing operations on columns. Therefore, Python and its Pandas library are used extensively in this stage. Complicated visualizations that plot based on geography (e.g chloropleth charts) are also created through Python's Matplotlib and GeoPandas.

Conclusion

The GE15 results demonstrated the deep divide between rural and urban voters, with the opposition coalition doing well in the urban areas, while the incumbent government's coalition was able to maintain its stronghold in the rural areas. It also highlighted the growing support for conservative Islamic parties. It is evident that ethnicity structure is still a remarkably strong predictor of election outcomes in the peninsula and it is likely that political uncertainty will persist in the coming months.
